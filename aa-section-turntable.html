    Section A.1 Derivation for a turntable

    Visualize a turntable of radius \(R\) that is rotating with constant angular velocity \(\vec{\Omega}\text{.}\) As shown in Figure A.1.1 below, \(\vec{\Omega}\) points perpendicular to the turntable and is directed out of the screen.
    🔗
    We can apply the local Cartesian coordinate system to any given point on the turntable, and for visual simplicity, we will do below so for a point on the edge of the turntable. All the unit direction vectors discussed below have an implied subscript "rot" since they are attached to the turntable, which is a rotating reference frame.
    🔗
    Consider a point fixed to the edge of the turntable: \(\hat{i}\) points to the local right, \(\hat{j}\) points directly inward toward the center of the turntable, and \(\hat{k}\) points in the same direction as \(\vec{\Omega}\text{.}\) Therefore, we can write \(\vec{\Omega} = \Omega \, \hat{k}\text{,}\) where \(\lambda\) [\(\text{rad}\)] is the angular displacement and \(\Omega = \dfrac{D \lambda}{Dt}\) [\(\text{rad} \, \text{s}^{-1}\)] is the angular speed. By these definitions, during a period of time \(\delta t\text{,}\) the turntable rotates through angular displacement \(\delta \lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that any point on the turntable can be produced simply by decreasing the value of \(R\text{;}\) more generally, any point on the same plane as the turntable can be produced simply by changing the value of \(R\text{.}\) Thus, the following derivations apply to any point in the plane of the turntable.
    🔗
    Furthermore, note that any point above or below the turntable can be produced simply by "lifting" the unit direction vectors upward in the direction of \(\vec{\Omega}\) or "pushing" the unit direction vectors downward in the direction of \(-\vec{\Omega}\text{,}\) respectively, as this does not change their orientations. Thus, the following derivations apply to any point above or below the plane of the turntable.
    🔗

    Subsection A.1.1 Expanding the total derivative of \(\hat{i}\)

    The orientations of the unit direction vectors of the local Cartesian coordinate system can change depending on where their anchoring point is placed on the turntable or above or below it. Furthermore, they and their point rotate along with the turntable. Consequently, \(\hat{i} = \hat{i}(\lambda, R, z)\text{,}\) \(\hat{j} = \hat{j}(\lambda, R, z)\text{,}\) and \(\hat{k} = \hat{k}(\lambda, R, z)\text{,}\) and \(\lambda = \lambda(t)\text{,}\) \(R = R(t)\text{,}\) and \(z = z(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We will start by using the chain rule to expand \(\dfrac{D \hat{i}}{Dt}\text{.}\) This will give us insight into how \(\hat{i}\) changes orientation as the turntable rotates.
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \hat{i}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial \lambda} \frac{D \lambda}{Dt} + \frac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial R} \frac{DR}{Dt} + \frac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial z} \frac{Dz}{Dt}\tag{A.1.1} \end{equation}
    🔗
    The second term on the right-hand side of (A.1.1) is \(\vec{0}\) since \(\hat{i}\) does not change orientation if placed at a different radius while holding \(\lambda\) and \(z\) constant, and therefore \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial R} = \vec{0}\text{.}\) Furthermore, the point does not move from the edge of the turntable, and so \(\dfrac{DR}{Dt} = 0\text{.}\) Likewise, the third term on the right-hand side of (A.1.1) is \(\vec{0}\) since \(\hat{i}\) does not change orientation if placed at a different height above or below the turntable while holding \(\lambda\) and \(R\) constant, and therefore \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial z} = \vec{0}\text{.}\) Furthermore, the point does not move above or below the edge of the turntable, and so \(\dfrac{Dz}{Dt} = 0\text{.}\) Therefore, (A.1.1) reduces to
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \hat{i}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial \lambda} \frac{D \lambda}{Dt}\tag{A.1.2} \end{equation}
    🔗
    To evaluate \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{i}}{\partial \lambda}\text{,}\) we consider the change in \(\hat{i}\)’s orientation when its anchoring point is fixed to the edge of the turntable and sweeps through angular displacement \(\delta \lambda\) over time \(\delta t\text{,}\) as shown in Figure A.1.2 below. \(\hat{i}_1\) is the initial position of \(\hat{i}\text{,}\) and \(\hat{i}_2\) is the final position of \(\hat{i}\) after time \(\delta t\) has passed and it has swept through angular displacement \(\delta \lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fixing the tails of \(\hat{i}_1\) and \(\hat{i}_2\) together in Figure A.1.3 below reveals that \(\delta \lambda\) is the angle swept out between them and \(\vec{\delta i}\) is the vector difference between them.
    🔗
    Reproducing Figure A.1.2 with the tail of \(\hat{i}_1\) moved to the tail of \(\hat{i}_2\) and \(\vec{\delta i}\) additionally included in Figure A.1.4 below makes these relationships even clearer.
    🔗
    We can use the arc length formula to approximate \(|\vec{\delta i}|\text{,}\) and we can take advantage of the fact that, since \(\hat{i}\) is a unit direction vector, \(|\hat{i}| = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} |\vec{\delta i}| = |\hat{i_1}| \, \delta \lambda = |\hat{i_2}| \, \delta \lambda = |\hat{i}| \, \delta \lambda = \delta \lambda\tag{A.1.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{|\vec{\delta i}|}{\delta t} = \frac{|\vec{\delta i}|}{\delta \lambda} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} = \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta \lambda} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} = \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t}\tag{A.1.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    From Figure A.1.4, we can see that as \(\delta t \to 0\text{,}\) \(\delta \lambda \to 0\) and \(\vec{\delta i}\) points directly inward toward the center of the turntable, which coincides with the \(\hat{j}\) direction. So
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \lim_{\delta t \to 0} \frac{\vec{\delta i}}{\delta t} = \frac{D \hat{i}}{D t} = \lim_{\delta t \to 0} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} \, \hat{j} = \frac{D\lambda}{Dt} \, \hat{j} = \Omega \, \hat{j}\tag{A.1.5} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A.1.2 Expanding the total derivative of \(\hat{j}\)

    We can use the chain rule to expand \(\dfrac{D \hat{j}}{Dt}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \hat{j}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial \lambda} \frac{D \lambda}{Dt} + \frac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial R} \frac{DR}{Dt} + \frac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial z} \frac{Dz}{Dt}\tag{A.1.6} \end{equation}
    🔗
    The second term on the right-hand side of (A.1.6) is \(\vec{0}\) since \(\hat{j}\) does not change orientation if placed at a different radius while holding \(\lambda\) and \(z\) constant, and therefore \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial R} = \vec{0}\text{.}\) Furthermore, as noted in Subsection A.1.1, the point does not move from the edge of the turntable, and so \(\dfrac{DR}{Dt} = 0\text{.}\) Likewise, the third term on the right-hand size of (A.1.6) is \(\vec{0}\) since \(\hat{j}\) does not change orientation if placed at a different height above or below the turntable while holding \(\lambda\) and \(R\) constant, and therefore \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial z} = \vec{0}\text{.}\) Furthermore, as noted in Subsection A.1.1, the point does not move above or below the edge of the turntable, and so \(\dfrac{Dz}{Dt} = 0\text{.}\) Therefore, (A.1.6) reduces to
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \hat{j}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial \lambda} \frac{D \lambda}{Dt}\tag{A.1.7} \end{equation}
    🔗
    To evaluate \(\dfrac{\partial \hat{j}}{\partial \lambda}\text{,}\) we consider the change in \(\hat{j}\)’s orientation when its anchoring point is fixed to the edge of the turntable and sweeps through angular displacement \(\delta \lambda\) over time \(\delta t\text{,}\) as shown in Figure A.1.5 below. \(\hat{j}_1\) is the initial position of \(\hat{j}\text{,}\) and \(\hat{j}_2\) is the final position of \(\hat{j}\) after time \(\delta t\) has passed and it has swept through angular displacement \(\delta \lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fixing the tails of \(\hat{j}_1\) and \(\hat{j}_2\) together in Figure A.1.6 below reveals that \(\delta \lambda\) is the angle swept out between them and \(\vec{\delta j}\) is the vector difference between them.
    🔗
    Reproducing Figure A.1.5 with the tail of \(\hat{j}_1\) moved to the tail of \(\hat{j}_2\) and \(\vec{\delta j}\) additionally included in Figure A.1.7 below makes these relationships even clearer.
    🔗
    Again, we can use the arc length formula to approximate \(|\vec{\delta j}|\text{,}\) and we can take advantage of the fact that, since \(\hat{j}\) is a unit direction vector, \(|\hat{j}| = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} |\vec{\delta j}| = |\hat{j_1}| \, \delta \lambda = |\hat{j_2}| \, \delta \lambda = |\hat{j}| \, \delta \lambda = \delta \lambda\tag{A.1.8} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{|\vec{\delta j}|}{\delta t} = \frac{|\vec{\delta j}|}{\delta \lambda} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} = \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta \lambda} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} = \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t}\tag{A.1.9} \end{equation}
    🔗
    From Figure A.1.7, we can see that as \(\delta t \to 0\text{,}\) \(\delta \lambda \to 0\) and \(\vec{\delta j}\) points to the local left, which coincides with the \(-\hat{i}\) direction. So
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \lim_{\delta t \to 0} \frac{\vec{\delta j}}{\delta t} = \frac{D \hat{j}}{D t} = \lim_{\delta t \to 0} \frac{\delta \lambda}{\delta t} (-\hat{i}) = -\frac{D\lambda}{Dt} \, \hat{i} = -\Omega \, \hat{i}\tag{A.1.10} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A.1.3 Expanding the total derivative of \(\hat{k}\)

    \(\hat{k}\) always points out of the screen no matter where it is placed in the plane of the turntable or above or below the turntable and no matter the angle through which the turntable rotates. Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \hat{k}}{Dt} = \vec{0}\tag{A.1.11} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A.1.4 Putting everything together for the turntable

    Given that \(\vec{\Omega} = \Omega \, \hat{k}\text{,}\) we can find \(\vec{\Omega} \times \hat{i}\text{,}\) \(\vec{\Omega} \times \hat{j}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{\Omega} \times \hat{k}\) and verify they match the results of (A.1.5), (A.1.10), and (A.1.11), respectively.
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{i} = \Omega \, \hat{j} = \frac{D \hat{i}}{Dt}\tag{A.1.12} \end{equation}
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{j} = -\Omega \, \hat{i} = \frac{D \hat{j}}{Dt}\tag{A.1.13} \end{equation}
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{k} = \vec{0} = \frac{D \hat{k}}{Dt}\tag{A.1.14} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Therefore, the derivation is complete.
