    Section 4.3 Rotating reference frame

    Consider a vector \(\vec{A}\) in three-dimensional space. Any coordinate system (e.g., Cartesian, local Cartesian, cylindrical, standard spherical) could serve as a reference frame to investigate \(\vec{A}\text{.}\) While the unit direction vectors of coordinate systems differ, and consequently the components of a given vector differ among coordinate systems, all coordinate systems can be used to describe the same vector.
    🔗
    Consider two Cartesian coordinate systems: One is non-accelerating, and the other is rotating relative to the first with constant angular velocity \(\vec{\Omega}\text{.}\) The former is an inertial reference frame and will be indicated by the subscript "abs". The latter is a non-inertial reference frame and will be indicated by the subscript "rot".
    🔗
    The inertial coordinate system is assigned unit direction vectors \(\hat{i}_{abs}\text{,}\) \(\hat{j}_{abs}\text{,}\) and \(\hat{k}_{abs}\text{,}\) and the rotating coordinate system is assigned unit direction vectors \(\hat{i}_{rot}\text{,}\) \(\hat{j}_{rot}\text{,}\) and \(\hat{k}_{rot}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In the inertial coordinate system
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{A} = A_{x,abs} \, \hat{i}_{abs} + A_{y,abs} \, \hat{j}_{abs} + A_{z,abs} \, \hat{k}_{abs}\tag{4.3.1} \end{equation}
    🔗
    and in the rotating coordinate system
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{A} = A_{x,rot} \, \hat{i}_{rot} + A_{y,rot} \, \hat{j}_{rot} + A_{z,rot} \, \hat{k}_{rot}\tag{4.3.2} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Both (4.3.1) and (4.3.2) are equally valid expressions for \(\vec{A}\text{:}\) Even though the values of their components differ, they each describe the same vector.
    🔗
    Let \(\vec{A}\) change with time, i.e., let \(\vec{A} = \vec{A}(t)\text{.}\) In the inertial coordinate system, which itself is not accelerating and therefore has unchanging unit direction vectors,
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} = \frac{D A_{x,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{i}_{abs} + \frac{D A_{y,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{j}_{abs} + \frac{D A_{z,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{k}_{abs}\tag{4.3.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    We call the expression on the right-hand side of (4.3.3) \(\left( \dfrac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{abs}\) since it describes how \(\vec{A}\) is changing with time relative to the inertial coordinate system:
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \left( \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{abs} = \frac{D A_{x,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{i}_{abs} + \frac{D A_{y,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{j}_{abs} + \frac{D A_{z,abs}}{Dt} \, \hat{k}_{abs}\tag{4.3.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    (4.3.3) and (4.3.4) make it clear that
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} = \left( \dfrac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{abs}\tag{4.3.5} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Since the rotating coordinate system itself accelerates, its unit direction vectors change orientation with time relative to the inertial coordinate system, and so by product rule,
    🔗
    \begin{align} \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} \amp = \frac{D A_{x,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{i}_{rot} + A_{x,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{i}_{rot}}{Dt}\notag\\ \amp \quad + \frac{D A_{y,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{j}_{rot} + A_{y,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{j}_{rot}}{Dt}\notag\\ \amp \quad + \frac{D A_{z,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{k}_{rot} + A_{z,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{k}_{rot}}{Dt}\notag\\ \amp = \left( \frac{D A_{x,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{i}_{rot} + \frac{D A_{y,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{j}_{rot} + \frac{D A_{z,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{k}_{rot} \right)\notag\\ \amp \quad + \left( A_{x,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{i}_{rot}}{Dt} + A_{y,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{j}_{rot}}{Dt} + A_{z,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{k}_{rot}}{Dt} \right)\tag{4.3.6} \end{align}
    🔗
    We call the first expression in parentheses on the right-hand side of (4.3.6) \(\left( \dfrac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{rot}\) since it describes how \(\vec{A}\) is changing relative to the rotating coordinate system:
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \left( \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{rot} = \frac{D A_{x,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{i}_{rot} + \frac{D A_{y,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{j}_{rot} + \frac{D A_{z,rot}}{Dt} \, \hat{k}_{rot}\tag{4.3.7} \end{equation}
    🔗
    But because of the second expression in parentheses on the right-hand side of (4.3.6), (4.3.6) and (4.3.7) make it clear that
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \vec{A}}{Dt} \neq \left( \dfrac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{rot}\tag{4.3.8} \end{equation}
    🔗
    The second expression on in parentheses on the right-hand side of (4.3.6) arises because the unit direction vectors rotate with their coordinate system at \(\vec{\Omega}\text{.}\) It follows from the definition of tangential velocity \(\vec{v}_T\) for uniform circular motion
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \vec{v_T} = \vec{\Omega} \times \vec{r} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{D \hat{i}_{rot}}{Dt} = \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{i}_{rot} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{D \hat{j}_{rot}}{Dt} = \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{j}_{rot} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{D \hat{k}_{rot}}{Dt} = \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{k}_{rot} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (The full derivation of these terms is shown in Appendix A.) Therefore, the second expression in parenthesis on the right-hand side of (4.3.6) becomes
    🔗
    \begin{align*} A_{x,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{i}_{rot}}{Dt} + A_{y,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{j}_{rot}}{Dt} + A_{z,rot} \, \frac{D \hat{k}_{rot}}{Dt} \amp = A_{x,rot} \left( \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{i}_{rot} \right) + A_{y,rot} \left( \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{j}_{rot} \right) + A_{z,rot} \left( \vec{\Omega} \times \hat{k}_{rot} \right)\\ \amp = \vec{\Omega} \times \left( A_{x,rot} \, \hat{i}_{rot} \right) + \vec{\Omega} \times \left( A_{y,rot} \, \hat{j}_{rot} \right) + \vec{\Omega} \times \left( A_{z,rot} \, \hat{k}_{rot} \right)\\ \amp = \vec{\Omega} \times \left( A_{x,rot} \, \hat{i}_{rot} + A_{y,rot} \, \hat{j}_{rot} + A_{z,rot} \, \hat{k}_{rot} \right)\\ \amp = \vec{\Omega} \times \vec{A} \end{align*}
    🔗
    where the scalar multiplication property of the cross product
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} k \left( \vec{a} \times \vec{b} \right) = \vec{a} \times \left( k \, \vec{b} \right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    as well as its distributive property over addition have been used (review these properties in Subsubsection 1.2.2.3).
    🔗
    Thus, (4.3.6) becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} = \left( \frac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{rot} + \vec{\Omega} \times \vec{A}\tag{4.3.9} \end{equation}
    🔗
    and equating (4.3.5) and (4.3.9) reveals
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \left( \frac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{abs} = \left( \frac{D\vec{A}}{Dt} \right)_{rot} + \vec{\Omega} \times \vec{A}\tag{4.3.10} \end{equation}
    🔗
    (4.3.10) says the time rate of change of an arbitrary vector \(\vec{A}\) relative to an inertial reference frame is due to the time rate of change of the vector relative to a rotating reference frame as well as to the rotation of that reference frame; in particular, it results from their sum.
