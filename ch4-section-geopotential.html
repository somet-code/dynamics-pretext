    Section 4.8 Geopotential and geopotential height

    Geopotential \(\Phi\) [\(\text{J} \, \text{kg}^{-1}\)] is defined as the mechanical work that must be done against gravity to lift a 1 kg mass (such as an air parcel) from Earth’s mean sea level to geometric height \(z\text{.}\) Alternatively, \(\Phi\) can be interpreted as the gravity potential energy per unit mass an object possesses at geometric height \(z\text{.}\) It follows that every geometric height \(z\) is associated with a unique value of \(\Phi\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Recall from your calculus and/or physics courses that the mechanical work \(W\) [J] done against a one-dimensional force \(F(x)\) to move an object from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation} W = \int_a^b F(x) \, dx\tag{4.8.1} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Since Newton’s \(2^{\text{nd}}\) law of motion tells us we can write the magnitude of a force as \(F = ma\text{,}\) (4.8.1) can be written
    🔗
    \begin{equation} W = \int_a^b m(x) \, a(x) \, dx\tag{4.8.2} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Assuming constant mass, (4.8.2) can be written
    🔗
    \begin{equation} W = m \int_a^b a(x) \, dx\tag{4.8.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    and so the work per unit mass (i.e., the specific work) \(w\) [\(\text{J} \, \text{kg}^{-1}\)] is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation} w = \frac{W}{m} = \int_a^b a(x) \, dx\tag{4.8.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Note that \(x\) is a dummy variable since it does not appear in the final integrated result. We could replace \(x\) with another variable, such as \(\gamma\) or \(\eta\) or \(\zeta\text{,}\) and the result would be the same.
    🔗
    We can apply (4.8.4) to the verbal definition of \(\Phi\) discussed above to produce its integral definition.
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \Phi = \int_0^z g(\zeta) \, d\zeta\tag{4.8.5} \end{equation}
    🔗
    where the dummy variable \(\zeta\) has been used for clarity.
    🔗
    Since \(\zeta\) "disappears" in the integration, (4.8.5) reveals that \(\Phi = \Phi(z)\text{.}\) It follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \nabla \Phi = \frac{d \Phi}{dz} \, \hat{k}\tag{4.8.6} \end{equation}
    🔗
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{d \Phi}{dz} = \frac{d}{dz} \int_0^z g(\zeta) \, d\zeta = g(z)\tag{4.8.7} \end{equation}
    🔗
    by the \(1^\text{st}\) part of the fundamental theorem of calculus. Since \(\vec{g} = -g \, \hat{k}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \vec{g} = -\nabla \Phi\tag{4.8.8} \end{equation}
    🔗
    and we can write (4.7.2) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{D \vec{u}}{Dt} = - 2\vec{\Omega} \times \vec{u} - \nabla \Phi - \frac{1}{\rho} \nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \vec{u}\tag{4.8.9} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Since surfaces of constant geopotential are parallel to Earth’s oblate spheroid surface, it can be useful to define geopotential height \(Z\) [m] for use rather than geometric height.
    🔗
    \begin{equation} Z = \frac{\Phi}{g_0}\tag{4.8.10} \end{equation}
    🔗
    where \(g_0 = g(0) = 9.81 \, \text{m} \, \text{s}^{-2}\) is the magnitude of the gravity acceleration at Earth’s mean sea level. You will see this value frequently, as it is a reasonable approximation to true gravity for heights in the troposphere relevant to the large majority of our weather.
    🔗
    Since \(\Phi = \Phi(z)\text{,}\) it follows from (4.8.10) that \(Z = Z(z)\text{,}\) and so every geometric height \(z\) is associated with a unique value of geopotential height\(Z\text{.}\) While both \(z\) and \(Z\) are measured in meters, the former may be referred to as "geometric meters" and the latter may be referred to as "geopotential meters" to clarify which measure of height is being used.
